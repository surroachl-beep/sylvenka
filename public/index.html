Sounds & script

Sylvenka has ten consonants, five pure vowels, and a rhythm you can learn in a minute.

The five vowels

Vowels are pure — no gliding, as in Spanish or Italian. Say each one exactly the same way every time.

VowelSounds likeHear it
a"ah" as in father
e"eh" as in bet
i"ee" as in machine
o"oh" as in note, but pure
u"oo" as in flute

The ten consonants

s, l, v, n, m, t, r, th, sh — plus silence, which Sylvenka poets jokingly count as the tenth. All are pronounced as in English; th is always soft as in think, never as in this, and r is lightly tapped as in Spanish pero.

Syllables and stress

Every syllable is a consonant plus a vowel (na-shu), though words may begin with a bare vowel (ovan, ithi, en). Nothing ever ends in a consonant cluster; the language flows like water over stones. Stress always falls on the second-to-last syllable: so-LO-va, na-SHU-en, ve-lo-RE-va.

Miren-sela lamaka ne solonu.
"Rain falls in the forest (I infer — the paths are wet)." Listen for the even, rolling rhythm.

The venashi script

Sylvenka is written in venashi — "seen-marks." Consonants are the base glyphs; vowels ride above them as small marks; and the four evidence markers get tall glyphs, so you can tell at a glance whether a page is testimony, rumor, deduction, or dream. Sacred poetry is traditionally written entirely in the looping dream-glyphs of -le.

The complete grammar

Everything Sylvenka does, on one page. Bookmark this — it is the whole rulebook.

1 · Word order

Subject – Object – Verb. Adjectives follow their noun. A possessor stands directly before what it owns. There are no articles and no grammatical gender.

Tovi lama ovan velova.
friend tree big see-WITNESSED — "My friend saw the big tree (I watched them see it)."

2 · Pronouns

SylvenkaMeaning
naI, me
siyou (one person)
thothey, that one (any gender)
nasiwe — you and I together (inclusive)
nathowe — me and others, not you (exclusive)
sisiyou all
thothothey (several)

Choosing the wrong "we" is Sylvenka's classic social blunder — nasi warmly includes your listener; natho politely closes the door.

3 · Nouns and plurals

Pluralize by doubling the first syllable: lama (tree) → lalama (trees); tovi (friend) → totovi (friends); mishi (bird) → mimishi (birds). Vowel-initial words double the vowel syllable: ovan → o-ovan, spoken with a gentle break.

4 · The verb: root + tense + evidence

The verb is Sylvenka's engine. It always ends with one of the four evidence markers, and tense slots in just before it.

SlotForms
Rootvelo see, nashu go, shila speak…
Tense (optional)-re- past · -mi- future · nothing = present
Evidence (required)-va witnessed · -shi hearsay · -nu inferred · -le dreamed/hoped
veloreva
see-PAST-WITNESSED — "I saw it, with my own eyes, back then."
nashureshi
go-PAST-HEARSAY — "they went, or so I'm told."
solominu
rain-FUTURE-INFERRED — "it will rain (the clouds tell me)."
velomile
see-FUTURE-DREAMED — "we will see it (I hope, I imagine)."

The future pairs naturally with -nu or -le — you cannot witness what hasn't happened. The forbidden form velomiva ("I will see it and I witnessed it") is the grammar of prophecy, used only by poets and considered either blasphemous or brilliant, depending on the century.

5 · To be, negation, imperative

No copula in the present: Lama ovan — "the tree is big." In past and future, use the verb eso: Lama ovan esoreva — "the tree was big (I saw it)."

Negation wraps the verb in the circumfix o-…-th, literally fencing it off: velova → ovelovath "I did not see it."

Commands take bare root + -sa (no evidence needed — you're creating the event, not reporting it): Nashusa! "Go!" · Velosa! "Look!"

Na tho oveloreVAth.
"I did not see them." Note the negative fence around the whole verb.

6 · Questions

Yes/no questions begin with the particle ve: Ve si nashureva? "Did you go?" Answer vaso (yes — "witnessed-true") or othath (no). Question words are built on ve-:

WordMeaningLiterally
vesiwhoquestion-person
venowhatquestion-thing
vekawherequestion-place
vemiwhenquestion-future/time
venuwhyquestion-inference
vesahowquestion-manner

7 · Postpositions and connectors

Little words come after their noun: selaka ne "at the lake," tovi lu "with a friend," vasa sa "toward home," lamaka ro "from the forest." Connectors: so and · vo or · tha but.

8 · Building new words

SuffixMakesExample

-enone who doesnashu → nashuen traveler · lovu → loven teacher

Compounds are head-final, read right to left: miren-sela "sky-water" = rain · soren-tovi "light-friend" = beloved · nolu-mishi "night-bird" = owl.

9 · Numbers — base five

Counted on one hand: en 1, ru 2, sam 3, vi 4, lush 5 ("a hand"). Then stack hands: lush-en 6, lush-vi 9, rulush 10, samlush-ru 17, lushlush 25 — "a hand of hands," the great round number. A 25th birthday is the true coming-of-age. Numbers follow the noun: mishi sam "three birds" (no plural doubling needed when a number is present).

en · ru · sam · vi · lush · lush-en · lush-ru · lush-sam · lush-vi · rulush
Counting one to ten.

10 · The culture inside the grammar

Sylvenka courts swear no oaths — testimony in -va is binding by itself, and false -va is the language's gravest taboo. Historians write in -shi and -nu, and the shift between them marks trust in a source. Children famously overuse -le: "there's a monster under the bed (dreamed)" is technically never a lie. And the four-line velasin poem must move through all four markers — from certainty to dream.

Move through these in order. Each builds on the last. Take your time — there's no rush in Sylvenka.
